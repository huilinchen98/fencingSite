Flicks

Some fencers hate flicks, simply hate them. You might as well hate the ocean. Flicks don't care if you hate them -- they simply score on you.

Flicks come in all shapes and sizes, behave very much like coups, and can be blocked easily using Sabre-style parries. This doesn't make them less frustrating for the recipients, or less painful when delivered by new fencers. In late 2004/early 2005, some changes to the Foil Rules by the FIE might make flicks more difficult, but they won't go away. If epee is any indication, flicks will still be possible.

The important thing about the flick is that it is situational, it works best given the proper distance, target position, and timing.

With straight extensions, if you extend your blade and walk towards the opponent, you will eventually hit them. This is not true of the flick;it requires a finishing hand move to bring the point to bear on the opponent's target. Until the flick is actually traveling to target, it is a vague threat and not an immediate threat. This factor allows good opponents to stop-hit against flick, and it causes not-so-good opponents to think they can stop-hit (and then complain to the director). Directors, by and large, give the flick the same protection as a coupe or cutover: Unless there is egregious multi-tempo pulling of the hand, the flick is considered a continuous threat to the target, because, well, it hit, didn't it?

Importantly, the flick bends the blade and requires an elevated hand: Your distance is much closer to hit with a flick than with a point attack. On the other hand, the flick is much more forgiving than a straight extension if you accidentally get too close.
