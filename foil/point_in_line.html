Point in Line

The Point in Line is the action we all want to love, but it always lets us down at the end and leaves us alone at the restaurant, trying to look like we want it that way. We've all gotten a few touches with it, but probably more than a few opponents have taken our lines and rammed them down our throats. It's never clear whether we're using the line, or the line is using us. Also unhelpful is the fact that the rules are vague in regards to the line. The arm must be extended and the tip "threatening target", but for how long? What are the feet doing? When does the extension become a line? What do directors want? These discussions, once started, are hard to stop.

The best way to think of the Point in Line is as a double-edged swordâ€¦ or better, a double-tipped weapon. It can be a threat to your opponent, but it can also be a threat to you. Because while using the line, your goal is transparent, you're usually immobile, and your arm is straight: You're singularly exposed to your opponent.

Tactically, the Point in Line works like a house with a sign that says: "This house protected by a home security alarm." It puts the fear on the opponent, it slows them down, it makes them stop and open their burgular's bag to pull out the anti-alarm tools. As a fencer, this is usually all you need, and you can use the hesitation to take over the attack or get away, whatever you need.

And, every so often, it works.
